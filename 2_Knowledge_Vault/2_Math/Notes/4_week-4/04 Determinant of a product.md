# Determinant of a Product

**Tags:** #math/determinants #math/singular_matrices #math/matrix_operations

## 💡 The Core Concept

The determinant of a product of matrices is equal to the product of their individual determinants. Mathematically, for any two square matrices $A$ and $B$:
$$ \det(AB) = \det(A) \cdot \det(B) $$

Geometrically, this rule is highly intuitive. A linear transformation represented by a matrix scales spatial areas (or volumes) by a factor equal to its determinant. When we chain transformations (i.e., perform matrix multiplication $AB$), we are applying one transformation after another. Therefore, the scale factors of these successive transformations must multiply.

---

## 🛠️ Mathematical Definitions & Mechanics

### 1. Algebraic Verification
Let us verify this property using the specific matrices from the lecture.

Define the transformations $A$ and $B$:
$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Calculate their individual determinants:
* Determinant of $A$:
  $$ \det(A) = (3 \cdot 2) - (1 \cdot 1) = 6 - 1 = 5 $$
* Determinant of $B$:
  $$ \det(B) = (1 \cdot 1) - (1 \cdot -2) = 1 + 2 = 3 $$

Now, calculate the product matrix $AB$:
$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (3 \cdot 1) + (1 \cdot -2) & (3 \cdot 1) + (1 \cdot 1) \\ (1 \cdot 1) + (2 \cdot -2) & (1 \cdot 1) + (2 \cdot 1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Calculate the determinant of the product $AB$:
$$ \det(AB) = (1 \cdot 3) - (4 \cdot -3) = 3 + 12 = 15 $$

Comparing the values:
$$ \det(AB) = 15 = 5 \cdot 3 = \det(A) \cdot \det(B) $$

The algebraic relation holds true.

---

### 2. Geometric Intuition
Chaining linear transformations means applying them sequentially to the coordinate space:
1. We start with a fundamental unit square of $\text{Area} = 1$.
2. Applying the first transformation, $B$, scales the area of the space by its determinant, $\det(B) = 3$. The unit square transforms into a parallelogram of $\text{Area} = 3$.
3. Applying the second transformation, $A$, scales *all* existing areas in the space by its determinant, $\det(A) = 5$.
4. The parallelogram of area $3$ is therefore scaled by $5$, resulting in a final area of:
   $$ 3 \times 5 = 15 $$

The composite transformation $AB$ maps the original unit square directly into a parallelogram of $\text{Area} = 15$, which matches $\det(AB)$.

> [!info] **Area Scaling is Independent of Shape**
> Any transformation scales *every* sub-area in the plane by its determinant, regardless of its shape. This is why the scaling factor of $5$ applies perfectly to the parallelogram of area $3$ generated by the first transformation.

### 3. The Singularity of Matrix Products
If at least one matrix in a product chain is singular, the entire product is singular.

#### Algebraic Proof:
Let $A$ be a non-singular matrix ($\det(A) \neq 0$) and $B$ be a singular matrix ($\det(B) = 0$). The determinant of their product is:
$$ \det(AB) = \det(A) \cdot \det(B) = \det(A) \cdot 0 = 0 $$
Because the determinant of the product is $0$, the matrix $AB$ must be singular.

#### Algebraic Example:
Consider $A$ (non-singular) and $B$ (singular):
$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} $$
$$ \det(A) = 5, \quad \det(B) = 0 $$

Compute the product $AB$:
$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (3 \cdot 1) + (1 \cdot 1) & (3 \cdot 2) + (1 \cdot 2) \\ (1 \cdot 1) + (2 \cdot 1) & (1 \cdot 2) + (2 \cdot 2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 8 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} $$

Compute the determinant of the product:
$$ \det(AB) = (4 \cdot 6) - (8 \cdot 3) = 24 - 24 = 0 $$

#### Geometric Explanation:
The singular transformation $B$ collapses the 2D plane onto a 1D line segment, which has an area of $0$. Applying $A$ subsequently to this line segment will rotate or stretch it, but it cannot restore it back into a 2D plane. Once dimension is collapsed (scaled by $0$), it remains collapsed.

> [!warning] **The "Zero" Property of Singular Matrices**
> Just as multiplying any scalar by $0$ yields $0$, multiplying any matrix by a singular matrix collapses the dimensionality of the final output space. Order does not matter; if any matrix in a sequence of operations is singular, the end transformation is singular.

---

## 🎯 Key Takeaways

* **Scale Chaining:** Chaining linear transformations multiplies their respective scaling factors: $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$. This holds true for any sequence of square matrices ($A_1 A_2 \dots A_n$).
* **Irreversible Dimension Loss:** If any step in a pipeline of linear operations collapses dimensions (i.e., is singular), the final output will remain collapsed. This is a critical factor in deep neural networks, where retaining rank across layers is essential to prevent information bottlenecks.
